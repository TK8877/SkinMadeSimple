Know What's In Your Products

Ingredients Decoded

From hyaluronic acid to retinol — learn what the most popular skincare ingredients actually do, who they're best for, and how to use them safely.

Hydrators & Humectants

Moisture-boosting ingredients.

Humectants are ingredients that draw water into the skin from the environment or deeper skin layers, helping keep the complexion plump and hydrated. These are some of the gentlest and most universally beneficial skincare ingredients available.

All Skin Types

Hyaluronic Acid

A powerhouse humectant that can hold up to 1,000 times its weight in water. Naturally occurring in the body, it plumps and hydrates the skin. Apply to damp skin and immediately follow with a moisturizer to lock in the hydration — without this step, it can actually draw moisture out of the skin in dry climates.

All Skin Types

Glycerin

One of the most effective and widely used humectants in skincare. Found in most moisturizers, toners, and cleansers. Glycerin is very gentle, non-irritating, and works for all skin types including sensitive skin. It pulls moisture from the air into the upper layers of skin, maintaining soft, supple texture throughout the day.

Dry / Normal

Squalane

A lightweight, stable oil derived from olives or sugarcane that closely mimics the skin's own natural sebum. It's non-comedogenic (won't clog pores), absorbs quickly, and is excellent for softening and protecting the skin barrier. A great option for those who want the nourishing benefits of an oil without heaviness.

Actives & Treatments

Targeted treatment ingredients.

Active ingredients are the workhorses of skincare — they deliver specific, measurable changes to the skin. However, they require more care when introducing them. Always start slowly, and never mix actives without researching their compatibility first.

Start Slow · PM Only

Retinol (Vitamin A)

Widely considered the gold standard anti-aging ingredient. Retinol speeds up cell turnover, stimulates collagen, reduces the appearance of fine lines, and improves skin texture over time. Begin with the lowest concentration (0.025–0.05%) and use only at night, every other night at first. Expect dryness and possible purging during the first 4–6 weeks as your skin adjusts.

Brightening · AM

Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid)

A potent antioxidant that brightens the complexion, fades hyperpigmentation and dark spots, boosts collagen production, and protects against free radical damage from UV and pollution. Best used in the morning before SPF. Look for formulas containing 10–20% L-ascorbic acid and store in a cool, dark place as Vitamin C oxidizes quickly when exposed to light and air.

All Skin Types

Niacinamide (Vitamin B3)

One of the most versatile skincare ingredients available. Niacinamide reduces redness and inflammation, visibly minimizes the appearance of pores, regulates sebum production for oily skin, fades dark spots, and strengthens the skin barrier. It works well with nearly all other ingredients and is gentle enough for daily use on even the most sensitive skin types.

Exfoliant · AHA

Glycolic & Lactic Acid

Alpha-hydroxy acids (AHAs) chemically exfoliate the surface of the skin by dissolving the bonds between dead skin cells, revealing smoother, brighter skin below. Glycolic acid is the strongest AHA and works fastest; lactic acid is gentler and better suited for sensitive or dry skin beginners. Use 1–3 times per week at night and always follow with SPF the next morning — AHAs increase sun sensitivity.

Exfoliant · BHA · Oily

Salicylic Acid

A beta-hydroxy acid (BHA) that is oil-soluble, allowing it to penetrate deep into pores and dissolve the excess sebum and dead skin cells that cause blackheads, whiteheads, and acne. This makes it uniquely effective for oily and acne-prone skin. Available in concentrations of 0.5–2%. Use 1–2 times per week to avoid over-exfoliation and barrier damage.

Barrier Repair · Dry

Ceramides

Ceramides are lipid molecules that make up approximately 50% of the skin's outer barrier layer. They act like the "mortar" between skin cells, keeping moisture locked in and irritants locked out. Ceramide levels naturally decline with age and can be depleted by over-cleansing or harsh actives. Replenishing them through moisturizers is especially important for dry, eczema-prone, or compromised skin.

Ingredient Conflicts

What not to mix.

Some ingredient combinations can irritate the skin, neutralize each other's effectiveness, or even cause damage. Knowing these pairs is just as important as knowing the individual ingredients.

🚫

Retinol + AHAs/BHAs

Layering retinol with chemical exfoliants causes significant irritation and can compromise the skin barrier. Alternate them on different nights — use exfoliants on Monday/Wednesday/Friday, retinol on others.

🚫

Vitamin C + Retinol

Both are potent actives. Together they can cause redness and irritation. The easy fix: use Vitamin C in the morning (with SPF) and Retinol exclusively at night.

⚠️

Vitamin C + Niacinamide

These two can reduce each other's effectiveness when used simultaneously. Apply Vitamin C first, let it absorb for 20–30 minutes, then follow with Niacinamide. Or use them at different times of day.

✅

Hyaluronic Acid + Everything

Hyaluronic acid is uniquely compatible with virtually all other ingredients — Vitamin C, retinol, niacinamide, exfoliants, and SPF. It's one of the few ingredients that works harmoniously with any routine.
