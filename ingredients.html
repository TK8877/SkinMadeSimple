✦ Know What's In Your Products

Ingredients Decoded

From hyaluronic acid to retinol — learn what key skincare ingredients actually do, who they're best for, and how to use them safely.

✦ Hydrators & Humectants

Moisture-drawing ingredients.

Humectants attract water into the upper layers of skin from both the environment and deeper tissue, keeping your complexion plump and supple. These are the gentlest and most universally beneficial ingredients — perfect entry points for any new routine.

All Skin Types

Hyaluronic Acid

A naturally occurring molecule that can hold up to 1,000× its weight in water. It plumps, hydrates, and temporarily smooths fine lines. Key tip: always apply to damp skin and immediately follow with a moisturiser — without this seal, HA can draw moisture out of the skin in dry conditions.

All Skin Types

Glycerin

One of the most effective and widely used humectants in skincare. Found in the majority of moisturisers, toners, and cleansers. Glycerin is gentle, non-irritating, and appropriate for all skin types — including sensitive and acne-prone skin. It attracts moisture from the air into the surface layers of skin for all-day softness.

Dry / Normal

Squalane

A lightweight, highly stable oil derived from olives or sugarcane that closely mimics the skin's own natural sebum. Absorbs quickly, is non-comedogenic, and excels at softening the skin barrier without feeling heavy or triggering breakouts. A reliable option for those wanting oil-like nourishment in a lightweight form.

✦ Actives & Treatments

Results-driven ingredients.

Active ingredients are the workhorses of skincare — they create real, measurable changes to the skin over time. They require careful introduction: always start with low concentrations, patch test first, and never combine actives without checking their compatibility.

Start Slow · PM Only

Retinol (Vitamin A)

The gold standard anti-ageing ingredient. Retinol accelerates cell turnover, stimulates collagen production, reduces fine lines and wrinkles, and improves overall skin texture. Begin at the lowest concentration (0.025–0.05%) every other night. Expect an adjustment phase of 4–6 weeks that may include temporary dryness and purging — this is normal and resolves with time.

Brightening · AM

Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid)

A potent antioxidant that brightens the complexion, fades dark spots and hyperpigmentation, boosts collagen synthesis, and defends skin against free-radical damage from UV and pollution. Best applied in the morning before sunscreen. Opt for 10–20% L-ascorbic acid formulas and store away from light and heat to prevent oxidation.

All Skin Types

Niacinamide (Vitamin B3)

One of the most versatile ingredients in skincare. Niacinamide reduces redness and inflammation, visibly minimises pores, regulates sebum production, fades dark spots, and strengthens the skin barrier — all in a single ingredient. Gentle enough for daily use on even the most reactive skin, and compatible with nearly every other active in your routine.

Exfoliant · AHA

Glycolic & Lactic Acid

Alpha-hydroxy acids that chemically dissolve the bonds holding dead skin cells to the surface, revealing smoother, brighter, more even-toned skin beneath. Lactic acid is gentler and better suited for dry or sensitive beginners. Use 1–3 times per week in the evening only. Always apply SPF the following morning — AHAs significantly increase UV sensitivity.

Exfoliant · BHA · Oily

Salicylic Acid

A beta-hydroxy acid (BHA) that is oil-soluble, allowing it to penetrate inside pores and dissolve the excess sebum and debris that cause blackheads, whiteheads, and breakouts. Uniquely effective for oily and acne-prone skin. Use at 0.5–2% concentration, 1–2 times per week. Do not layer with retinol on the same night.

Barrier Repair

Ceramides

Lipid molecules that form roughly 50% of the skin's outer barrier layer. They act as the "mortar" between skin cells, locking moisture in and keeping irritants out. Ceramide levels decline naturally with age and can be depleted by over-cleansing or harsh actives. Ceramide-rich moisturisers are especially vital for dry, eczema-prone, or barrier-compromised skin.

✦ Ingredient Conflicts

What not to mix.

Some ingredient pairings reduce efficacy, cause irritation, or damage the skin barrier. Knowing these combinations is just as important as understanding each ingredient on its own.

🚫

Retinol + AHAs / BHAs

Combining retinol with chemical exfoliants in the same routine causes significant irritation and barrier compromise. Alternate by night — use AHAs/BHAs on Monday, Wednesday, Friday; retinol on Tuesday, Thursday.

🚫

Vitamin C + Retinol

Both are potent actives that increase irritation risk when combined. The simple fix is also the best practice anyway: Vitamin C in the morning (it's an antioxidant — sunlight exposure is where it excels), retinol at night.

⚠️

Vitamin C + Niacinamide

These two can diminish each other's effectiveness when applied at the same time. Apply Vitamin C first, let it absorb for 20–30 minutes, then apply Niacinamide — or simply use them at different times of day.

✅

Hyaluronic Acid + Everything

HA is universally compatible with virtually all other skincare ingredients. Vitamin C, retinol, niacinamide, exfoliants, oils, SPF — HA pairs well with all of them and is one of the safest ingredients to layer freely.

✦ Beginner Timeline

When to introduce actives.

As a beginner, resist the urge to try everything at once. Spend the first 4–6 weeks using only your core three: cleanser, moisturiser, and SPF. Let your skin establish a healthy baseline before introducing anything targeted.

Once your barrier feels comfortable and stable, add one active at a time — starting with something gentle like niacinamide or a low-percentage hyaluronic acid serum. Wait 2 weeks between each addition so you can accurately track what's working.

  • Weeks 1–6: Cleanser, moisturiser, SPF only
  • Week 7+: Add niacinamide or HA serum
  • Week 12+: Consider Vitamin C or low-dose retinol
  • Always: Patch test before applying to your full face
